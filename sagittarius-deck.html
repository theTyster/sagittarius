A field report

When
“looks correct”
isn’t.

How a machine for checking work caught a mistake that every human reviewer would have waved through — including the expert who built it.

SAGITTARIUS · an experiment in earning trust from AI-generated work

The problem

Plausible is not the same as correct.

Modern AI is very good at producing work that looks right — code, proofs, analysis that reads cleanly and passes a glance.

But the hard part was never generating the work. It’s knowing whether the work is actually true. A confident, well-formatted, completely wrong answer is the most dangerous thing a machine can hand you.

So the real question is: how do you verify work — without simply trusting the same intuition that produced it?

An analogy that already worked

How they photographed a black hole.

To image a black hole, the Event Horizon Telescope faced the same trap: the data was faint and the team already knew what a black hole should look like. Wishful analysis could easily “find” the expected picture in noise.

Their defense was structural independence. Several teams were sealed off from one another and reduced the same data separately. Only at the very end did they compare. When isolated teams converge on the same image, the image is trustworthy — because no one could have nudged anyone else toward the expected answer.

That bias-defense-by-independence is the whole idea. The project is named Sagittarius, after the black hole at our galaxy’s center that they imaged this way.

What we built

The same idea, for checking work.

Instead of one clever AI doing everything, we use a plain, predictable script that hands a problem to many independent AI specialists:

  • One specialist does the work.
  • Others, working separately, try their hardest to tear it down — to prove it wrong.
  • The work is also translated into actual mathematics and machine-checked, the way a calculator can’t lie about arithmetic.
  • Nothing passes unless it survives all of that.

We call the orchestrating script a workflow. Its job is to run the steps in a fixed order — not to have opinions.

The one rule

The clerk keeps score. The experts make the calls.

There is exactly one line the whole design exists to protect: the script does the bookkeeping — what runs next, what’s been done, reading off results — but it never makes a judgment itself.

“Is this proven? Refuted? Sound?” always belongs to the specialists. The moment the bookkeeping starts quietly deciding those things, you’ve lost the independence that made the answer trustworthy.

We named that failure the Orbital Inversion — a judgment that should have been handed off, but wasn’t. Watch for it later; it held under pressure.

The experiment

Is this elaborate machine actually worth it?

Fair question. Maybe a careful expert could just… do the checking themselves, and skip the whole apparatus. So we ran a clean test to find out:

  • 1. Predict. A careful engineer wrote down, in advance, exactly what they believed the machine would find — confidence levels and all. We sealed it.
  • 2. Run. Then we turned the machine loose on the same problem.
  • 3. Compare. Prediction vs. reality. The gap between them is the machine’s value.

If the machine only confirms what the expert already foresaw, it’s expensive theatre. If it finds what the expert missed, it’s earning its keep.

The task we set

Teach the system a new trick — and guarantee it can’t break anything.

We added a new capability to our own system (a faster way to start a job partway through, instead of always from the beginning).

The catch: we wanted a mathematical guarantee that the shortcut could never let the system skip a step it actually needed. A safety rail, proven — not hoped.

So the machine’s job was to take that promised guarantee and either prove it or show us why it can’t.

The sealed prediction

What the expert bet would happen.

The engineer’s honest forecast, written before the run:

  • high  The early stages would go smoothly and cleanly.
  • likely  The system would notice the guarantee was “thin” and reach for a stronger version of it.
  • low  The final proof would close neatly. (Hedged — “this is where I most expect to be wrong.”)

Reasonable, specific, and — as it turned out — right about the direction and wrong about the things that mattered.

What it found

The proof proved nothing.

Our “guarantee” was built from two checks that were supposed to be different: “Is the file there?” and “Does it load cleanly?”

The machine discovered these were secretly the exact same question, written two different ways. So “both must pass” really meant “one must pass — twice.” The dangerous case we wanted to rule out couldn’t even be described in the proof.

our two “different” safety checks were  identical

The guarantee guaranteed nothing. And it compiled. It looked completely fine.

Why that one matters

A green light that’s wired to always be on.

A safety check that passes no matter what isn’t a safety check — it’s a green light soldered to “on.” It tells you nothing, while looking exactly like reassurance.

This is the failure a human reviewer almost never catches. The proof builds. The tests are green. Everything reads as correct. Nobody re-derives the logic from scratch at 5pm on a Friday.

Only something whose only job is to attack the work bothered to ask: “does this check actually rule anything out?” The answer was no.

And it kept going

Three more real defects — all hiding behind “green.”

A backwards safety rail

A second guarantee we wrote down was not just weak — it was false as written. The machine produced a concrete counterexample.

A silent inconsistency

A data file loaded without error but referred to things it never defined — typos and renames that “passed” every surface check.

A broken bridge

The careful test we built on one side didn’t actually correspond to what the math on the other side was claiming.

The pattern

Every one looked fine. The file loaded; the proof built; the test was green. Green ≠ correct.

Why we trust the verdict

It refused to fake it — twice, independently.

Two specialists reached the “this proves nothing” conclusion separately, from different angles — the convergence-by-independence that made the black-hole image trustworthy.

And when one specialist couldn’t honestly close the proof, it said so — it refused to reach for a cheap trick that would turn the light green without earning it. It reported “I can’t prove this, and here’s exactly why,” and handed the problem back.

The bookkeeping never overrode that. The one rule held: the script kept score; the experts made every call.

Prediction vs. reality

Right about the direction. Wrong about the depth.

What the expert got right

The shape of the run. That the guarantee would need strengthening. The general arc of every stage.

What only the machine got

The specific bug that made the proof empty. The false safety rail. The silent data inconsistency. The broken bridge between test and math.

The value wasn’t the headline the expert already saw coming. It was the depth underneath it — the concrete, shippable mistakes a confident human would have signed off on.

In fairness: we even tipped the machine off toward the answer on one step. It still went deeper than the tip — and the un-tipped specialist reached the same verdict on its own.

Why it matters

“It looks right” is about to stop being good enough.

As we hand AI more work that actually matters, the gap between plausible and correct becomes the whole ballgame. The reassuring, well-formatted, subtly-empty answer is the real risk.

This is a way to make AI work earn trust instead of asking for it — by surviving independent scrutiny, adversarial attack, and machine-checked math, with a hard line between who keeps score and who makes the call.

On a problem an expert had already reasoned through carefully, the machine still caught four real mistakes they’d have shipped. That’s the case for the apparatus.

Where this stands

An honest experiment — and a clear result.

It’s early, and deliberately small in scope. But the test it was built to answer came back unambiguous: the machine found, in a verifiable way, what a careful human missed.
